Diagnostics.

The first attempt earned ten weekly users. This is the design work that earned the second one a place on the page — the wireframes I scrapped, the components I argued for, and the calls that shipped.

Whatfix · 2024, written in 2026 · Senior PD · 0→1, second attempt · Phase 1 shipped

Diagnostics is a passive troubleshooting panel that lives inside Preview Mode — a feature within Whatfix Studio, and the previewing surface creators reached for more than any other. Placing diagnostics there wasn't a design opinion; it was a research finding. What followed was a design job: several iteration cycles over roughly five months, a play-driven model killed in week eight, an argument with engineering over question-framed answers versus an attribute table, and a status pill the design system didn't yet support. This case study walks the design decisions, not just the outcomes.

Ten weekly active users.

10 on a platform with thousands of seats. The chart was on page two of the planning doc, where bad news goes when leadership has chosen to keep funding the problem anyway.

The year-old Content Inspector beta floated in the corner of Studio, listing one flow at a time. It was unusable for anyone who hadn't been trained on it. The brief I picked up in early 2024 read like a respec of the same beta. The first design job was to throw out the brief.

The brief was off by an audience.

Eight weeks of interviews and ticket reads turned up a number that changed who the product was for.
32 hrs
Internal RCA time per ticket — Support analysing and looping with the customer.
182
Monthly tickets in the content-not-displaying category.
4
Ways to preview content existed in Whatfix — Preview Mode, staging URLs, the dev-console command, publish-and-check. Only one was used at scale.

The brief had been pitched as a customer experience improvement. The faster pitch was internal cost — at 182 monthly tickets averaging 32 hours each, Support was burning 30 person-weeks per quarter on RCA the product should have been doing. Two personas surfaced: Muriel (reasons in symptoms), Efraim (reasons in coverage). Both questions needed answers. Neither was getting one.

The research also answered a question the brief hadn't asked: where in the workflow do creators currently check their work? Preview Mode — a feature inside Whatfix Studio for verifying content before publishing — was the previewing method creators used more than any of the other three combined. Staging URLs were rarely set up. The dev-console command was Support's tool, not the creator's. Publish-and-check was the fallback nobody wanted to admit to. Preview Mode was where the checking already happened. That finding reframed the surface question before the concepts were even drawn.

Four jobs came out of these interviews. Muriel needs to know why in creator language, and to verify her work before publishing. Efraim needs to see coverage across pages at a glance. Support needs the diagnosis handed over, not asked to run it. Phase 1 answers the first two. Phase 2 addresses Efraim's. Support benefits downstream from both.

framing · how might we

Three how-might-we's on the wall.

Each one implied a different product. Only one was shippable in a quarter.

  1. How might we help a creator resolve any technical issue faster — reducing the time and effort to resolve it?

  2. How might we help a creator find the issue and resolve it in the moment of noticing?

  3. How might we notify a creator about failures and guide them to resolve them — before they escalate?

We picked the second — find and resolve faster, in the moment of noticing. The NPS verbatims said creators were already there when failures happened; they just couldn't tell what had gone wrong. HMW 1 required a broader troubleshooting suite than the quarter would fit. HMW 3 required a monitoring layer we didn't yet have. HMW 2 was the shape of a Phase-1-shaped product.

framing · the design bet

The 32-hour path, and the path we designed for.

Current-state and future-state flows, drawn before the concepts. The design bet was collapsing the middle from days to minutes.

Current state

2023 · when a creator notices a failure ~32 hrs
  1. Creator notices content isn't displaying as expected.
  2. Tries to reproduce; can't isolate the cause.
  3. Files a Support ticket with what they can describe.
  4. Waits for Support to schedule a screen-share.
  5. Support engineer runs _wfx_debug_panel() in the creator's browser.
  6. Support reads the console output aloud; creator watches.
  7. Creator edits offline based on what they heard.
  8. Publishes to verify; catches surprises in production.

Path we designed for

2024 · what Phase 1 had to enable ~minutes
  1. Creator notices content isn't displaying as expected.
  2. Enables Preview Mode inside Studio.
  3. Panel indexes the page automatically, surfaces failed items.
  4. Creator scans statuses, opens detail on the failed item.
  5. Reads the question-framed answer in creator language.
  6. Edits inline, still inside Preview.
  7. Verifies in Preview; publishes when green.

Steps 3 through 7 of the current path — the escalation, the wait, the debug command, the screen-share, the offline edit — collapse into one action: open the panel. Everything else was creator behavior we already had. The design job was to earn the panel's place in that gesture.

Four concepts, tested before code.

Preview Mode was the surface. The concepts tested how diagnostics lived inside it — and what alternatives, on other surfaces, would have to beat it to move the answer.
A

Preview Mode · passive

Passive panel inside Studio's Preview Mode. Indexes all five content types on the current page.

B

Preview Mode · play-driven

Step-level diagnostics as a flow runs, also inside Preview Mode. Sharpest for Flows; blind to the other four content types.

C

Studio editor · ticketing

In-editor Report Issue with bundled screen recording and console logs. Speeds Support, not creators.

D

Dashboard · coverage

Global failure list for admins. Coverage view for Efraim's persona; abstract from the moment of failure.

Five to seven enterprise customers tested each concept as clickable prototypes. Concept A advanced — the passive panel inside Preview Mode, backing up what the research had already said: place diagnostics where creators already went to check their work. C's screen-capture ticketing was retained as a Phase 2 fast-follow. B and D shaped the backlog for later phases. The strongest concept-test finding: creators read in-context reasoning as a sentence and reached for the edit affordance — the phrasing was the affordance.

design · iteration 0

v0 was wrong. Here's how I knew.

Concept A had won the surface. Inside Preview Mode, the first build direction was play-driven — sharper for Flows and quicker to demo. Three sprints in, that direction was covering one of the five content types and none of the other four.

I had v0 in Figma. Open the panel, start a flow, watch each step mark pass or fail with the failure reason inline. The Flow prototype from the concept test looked right. The pixels were working.

I sat with Smart Tips for an hour trying to find the play state. There wasn't one. Smart Tips fire on triggers — segment match, URL match, time on page — they're configured, not played. The same was true of Beacons, Launchers, and Pop-ups. The play model only reached Flows.

The critique I wrote on my own work that afternoon: v0 forces a model that only one of the five content types fits. Same failure as Content Inspector — build for the loudest type, assume the others follow.

The call
Scrap v0. Rebuild as a passive page-keyed inspector.
Pushback · Eng
Three sprints in. Proposed shipping v0 with passive v2 as fast-follow.
How I held
Showed the critique frame above and the Content Inspector adoption chart side by side. v0 + fast-follow had been Content Inspector's exact pitch. The fast-follow had never come.
Cost
Three sprints rewritten. Six weeks of timeline absorbed.
design · iteration 1 → ship

The rewrite — sketch to ship in four steps.

The passive panel went from whiteboard to Figma to shipped in six weeks. Four iteration stops along the way.

The rewrite was a passive inspector keyed to the current page: open the panel, see what's there, see what isn't. The creator's job is to read, not perform. Four design problems had to be solved on the way to shipping: information architecture (what order do five content types appear in?), tab structure (what questions does the panel answer?), status pill (how do five states fit a three-state component?), and the detail panel (sentence, or attribute table?). Each one was its own argument.

design · information architecture

What order do you list five things in?

The passive panel needed to list five content types. The order is a design decision. Three orderings tested in three days, with the same prototype and three different content-list orders.

option 1

Alphabetical

Beacons, Flows, Launchers, Pop-ups, Smart Tips. Predictable but uncorrelated with how creators reason about their work.

option 2

By creation frequency

Smart Tips first, Pop-ups last. Matches platform-wide data but breaks when a customer's content mix is unusual.

option 3

By creator hierarchy

Flows first (the headline product), then Smart Tips, Beacons, Launchers, Pop-ups in declining specificity. Matches how creators describe their work in interviews.

Three test creators were asked to find a specific Smart Tip in each ordering. Time-to-find for the creator-hierarchy ordering was the lowest, by about 40%. The alphabet didn't tell a story. The frequency depended on the customer. The hierarchy matched how creators already think and is what shipped.

design · navigation

Four tabs, then two.

v1.2 had four tabs: Draft, Ready, Published, All. This matched the existing workflow stages in Whatfix. It was also the wrong tab structure for diagnostics.

Diagnostics asks one of two questions: is what's here working? or what should be here but isn't? Workflow stage is orthogonal to either. A piece in Draft can work perfectly. A piece in Published can be failing.

→

The two-tab structure is also the answer to the IA test: each tab maps to one question a creator asks. The workflow filter moved to a chip in the top right that defaults to All Stages. Power users can slice within a tab; everyone else doesn't have to think about it.

design · component

Three states became five, and the pill had to keep up.

The existing Whatfix design-system pill supported three states: Active, Inactive, Draft. Diagnostics needed five: Displaying, Not displaying — rule mismatch, Not displaying — element missing, Error, Loading. Three iterations.

The design-system team couldn't absorb a five-state pill mid-overhaul. I extended sideways with shape variation as a tactical workaround and filed the gap for the next contribution cycle. The DS team picked it up two months after launch and merged a five-state primitive that retroactively covered Diagnostics. The right escalation, not the right component, was the move that worked.

design · the longest argument

The detail panel argument.

Engineering wanted an attribute table. Three iterations and thirty printed tickets later, we shipped a sentence.

The shipped panel asks When does this appear? and answers in plain language. The raw attribute state — selector, role tag, segment ID — sits inside an accordion below, auto-opens for internal Support roles, stays collapsed for creators. Same data, two readers, one component.

The hard part was defending it in design review. Engineering's case for v0 was airtight: faithful to the system, single source of truth, no translation layer to maintain. I needed an argument that wasn't creators are confused by attributes — that argument I had already lost twice.

I pulled thirty actual Support tickets from the previous quarter and printed the first sentence of each one. In the design review I read the first three aloud — Why isn't my smart tip showing on the homepage?, When does this popup fire?, Where do I make this flow appear on mobile? — and then asked which of them an attribute table would answer. Zero. The attribute table optimised for the source of truth no creator was asking to read.

Engineering signed off in the meeting. The translation layer that powers v2 — one author-written sentence per state per content type — is a maintenance liability I named in the same review. It is on the Phase 2 backlog as swap hand-authored translations for LLM-generated, with a quality floor.

design · feature origin

The tab that came from one usability session.

Session three of the concept test. A creator was looking at the v1 panel and asked, out loud, "but where's the smart tip that should be in this corner?" She knew it had existed. It wasn't showing. The panel only listed what was on the page. That was the moment "Not on this page" became a tab.

Three placements for the missing-content surface:

placement 1

Filter toggle

"Show missing" toggle on the same list. Lower information scent; missing-ness reads as a state on existing items, not its own category.

placement 2

Sibling tab

Equal weight to "on this page". Two questions, two tabs — symmetric and discoverable.

placement 3

Separate panel

A different surface entirely. Too much navigation for an answer that lives next to its sibling.

The sibling tab won because the questions are symmetric. A filter would have hidden the feature from the creators who needed it most — the ones who didn't yet know to look for it.

The empty state was its own design problem. What if everything that should be on the page is on the page? The tab shows one line: All content is on this page. Secondary line: Last checked just now. No illustration. No celebration. Success is the absence of friction, not a moment to mark.

design · onboarding

One tooltip. Three placements. Three drafts.

The team agreed on one anchored tooltip for the first run. No tour. The placement was the first decision.

anchor 1

Corner badge

Pulse on the panel toggle. Caught attention. Creators didn't know what the badge meant.

anchor 2

Inside the open panel

Tooltip appearing once the panel was open. Required the creator to open it first, which defeated the introduction.

anchor 3

On the toggle, panel closed

Tooltip on the affordance, before any click. All five test creators understood it.

The copy was the harder problem.

Draft 1
"Diagnostics — your new troubleshooting tool". Jargon. Hides the action behind a name.
Draft 2
"Find out why your content isn't displaying". Assumes the problem. Reads as accusatory on the first open.
Draft 3 · shipped
"Diagnostics. Open this when something doesn't look right." The phrase "doesn't look right" was harvested verbatim from interviews — it's how three creators in a row described the failure they wanted help with.
design · behaviour

How the panel behaves.

States, motion, and accessibility — decisions made early, not retrofitted.

Four states designed before the happy path: empty (no content on the page), loading (first paint of a heavy page), dense (a page with twenty Smart Tips), and error (the panel can't read the page DOM). Each one has its own composition.

The dense state was the design problem that nearly delayed shipping. Twenty Smart Tips on a single page is rare but real — Salesforce dashboards in particular. The list virtualises after item twelve, with a sticky x of y · jump to failed control pinned to the bottom of the panel. Failed items are anchored to the top of their own section so scanning starts at what's broken, not at what's working.

Color-and-shape encoding for the status pill (above) is the visible part of the accessibility work. The invisible part is the screen-reader copy, which is a sentence rather than a flat attribute string — written so a non-sighted creator gets the same information density as a sighted one without having to traverse five labels in a row. The formal WCAG 2.2 audit is queued for the Phase 2 pre-release; compliance is currently asserted by the team, which is named in the gap list below.

What shipped, end of 2024.

Phase 1 lives inside Studio's Preview Mode as a right-side drawer. Two tabs, five content types, creator-language status, question-framed detail. The panel opens when the creator enables Preview Mode from Studio — the same gesture they already used to check their work, now with an answer key attached. The component is composed entirely from existing patterns: the drawer is Studio's, the pills are Analytics', the accordion is Workflows'. Zero net-new UI surfaces for customers to learn.

What I'd unship.

  1. The Diagnose CTA. It opens a panel that answers why, well — and doesn't help a creator fix. Rename to Inspect, or add the missing affordance behind it.

  2. The status pill workaround. Shape variation was the right tactical move and the wrong long-term primitive. The design-system pill that landed two months later is what should have shipped on day one — co-design with the DS pod earlier next time.

  3. The first-run tooltip on every account. It fires for power users who don't need it. Should detect prior Preview Mode usage and suppress.

the 2026 lens

What I'd design instead today.

The panel is the right component for 2024. In 2026 the component is no longer the product.
2024 — on-demand→ Continuous

Background watcher. Surface issues to the creator before they suspect. The panel opens because the system invited it.

2024 — point and click→ Conversational

Why isn't my onboarding showing for new sales reps? A creator asks; the system reasons across content, segments, and rules.

2024 — per-item→ Cross-content

Reason across the journey, not the asset. This Flow fails because the Smart Tip before it stopped firing.

2024 — open a ticket→ The diagnosis is the ticket

Support reads the agent's analysis instead of running their own. The 32-hour number stops being a Support problem.

The 2024 product was a panel. The 2026 product is an agent that uses a panel when it needs one. The LLM-generated reasoning layer I deferred to Phase 2 is the foundation of all of it — and it would be the foundation, not the optional enhancement, if I were starting today.

What I'd close before Phase 2.

  1. The LLM reasoning layer. Hand-authored translations were the right Phase 1 quality floor. They are the wrong Phase 2 default. Migrating now, with deterministic verification as the audit trail.

  2. Performance against real customer pages. 200ms first-paint budget met on internal fixtures. Untested against thousand-node DOM trees and heavy iframe pages. Outstanding.

  3. WCAG 2.2 audit, third-party. Compliance is asserted by the team. The audit is the cheapest risk reduction available.

  4. Efraim's coverage view, validated with the same depth. Most usability testing went to the Muriel side. The Dashboard coverage product needs its own concept and usability work before commit.

What I took from it.

Three habits this project taught me that travelled to every project after. Test the concept before you build the MVP — concept testing is the cheapest insurance in the design process and Whatfix's first attempt at this tool was the cost of skipping it. Defend the design with the user's own words — the thirty printed tickets won an argument that frame-by-frame critique had been losing for two weeks. And bring the design-system team in before the workaround, not after — the five-state pill I shipped sideways was the right tactic and the wrong long-term move.

The harder takeaway is the one this case study is still writing. The product I shipped is correct for 2024 and insufficient for 2026. The work that should never stop is looking at what shipped through the lens of what is now possible, and asking whether the answer that was right two years ago is still the right answer now.

Diagnostics is the artefact that taught me to write a case study. The 2026 lens is the one I am still writing.
