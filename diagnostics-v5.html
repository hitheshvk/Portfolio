Diagnostics.

Whatfix authors couldn't debug their own content. Four preview modes existed; none of them told an author what was wrong. This is the tab I designed inside one of them — built around the constraint that different content types can't be diagnosed the same way.

Whatfix · 2024, written in 2026 · Senior PD · troubleshooting tool · Phase 1 shipped

Diagnostics is a dedicated tab inside Whatfix Preview Mode. Enabling Preview Mode navigates the author to it. Content is categorized the same way as in creation — Flows, Smart Tips, Beacons, Launchers, Pop-ups — with a Not on this page section for content that belongs elsewhere. Underneath, three detection strategies run in parallel because flows need to be played to fail, smart tips can be surfaced upfront, and pop-ups need care to avoid false positives. One UI. Three logics.

The team: a designer (me), a PM, an EM, a UX researcher on-call, and the Support team as a discovery partner — they classified every ticket into what an author could fix and what they couldn't, which shaped the whole panel. Cross-pod partners in Engineering and the Design System pod.

[author's brief · Q1 2024 discovery]

There was no tool for authors.

When authors' content stopped working, the only path was escalation. There wasn't a design problem yet — there was a missing product.

When I picked up the brief in early 2024, Whatfix had no author-facing diagnostic tool. If a Smart Tip stopped firing or a Flow broke mid-step, the author had one option: file a Support ticket, wait for a screen-share, and watch a Whatfix engineer run a debug command in the developer tools of their own browser. That was the author-facing model for troubleshooting: escalate, wait, watch.

32 hrs
Internal RCA time per ticket — Support analysing and looping with the customer.
182
Monthly tickets in the content-not-displaying category.
4
Preview modes existed in Whatfix — all showing pixels, none showing failures.

The second discovery was inside the product. Whatfix had four different preview modes, each doing an overlapping version of the same job: showing an author what their content looked like before publish. Authors tried multiple methods, unsure which was right for what.

1

Preview Mode

In-Studio preview on the target URL, with editing enabled. The one authors reached for most.

2

See Live

Opens the customer's live product with content overlaid — in a separate view, disconnected from Studio.

3

Preview

Modal from the content list. Fast, but no target-URL context — URL-scoped diagnostics would be meaningless.

4

Launch Live Edit

For editing live production content. Wrong intent — this isn't the pre-publish check flow.

Why Preview Mode won. Not because it was popular — because it was the only mode structurally capable of hosting Diagnostics. Diagnosis, fix, and re-verify had to happen in one gesture, without leaving the surface. Preview Mode was the only one that ran in Studio, on the target URL, with editing enabled. See Live broke the loop with a separate view. Preview lacked URL context. Launch Live Edit was for the wrong moment in the workflow. The criterion decided the surface: never leave the surface to complete the loop.

[discovery · with the PM and the Support team · Q1–Q2 2024]

Who owns which issue?

The Support team knew the answer better than anyone else in the building. We sat with them and two quarters of tickets, and sorted every issue into two buckets. Their classification was the load-bearing part.

Support wasn't a downstream stakeholder to inform; they were the discovery partner. They'd resolved every one of the 182 monthly tickets themselves and knew, ticket by ticket, whether the author could have fixed it if they'd been shown how — or whether it genuinely needed engineering. The design's whole audience question — who is Diagnostics for? — was answered in that room, not in mine.

bucket 1 · author-fixable

Issues the author owns — surface them clearly.

  • Display rule mismatch (segment / URL / device)
  • Wrong or missing element selector
  • Content in wrong workflow stage
  • Occurrence rules not set correctly
  • Content published to the wrong page
bucket 2 · technical

Issues Support owns — smooth the escalation.

  • Browser extension version mismatches
  • Customer-side DOM structure changes
  • iframe permission and cross-origin failures
  • SPA route changes breaking anchors
  • CSP restrictions blocking Whatfix runtime
support brought
The classification itself — the ticket-by-ticket knowledge of which issues authors could actually own. Also the trust that a self-service tool wouldn't shove work back onto them; if the design got Bucket 2 wrong, Support would inherit the noise.
PM brought
The scope question. How much of Bucket 1 does Phase 1 have to cover to earn the launch? We agreed on the top five issue types by ticket volume — the display-rule, selector, stage, occurrence, and page-scoping issues shown left.
I brought
The UI reconciliation. Bucket 1 gets the fix path in the panel. Bucket 2 gets a clean escalation with the diagnosis pre-attached, so Support inherits context, not translation work.
we agreed
Neither bucket could feel like a dead end. Bucket 1 was the design job. Bucket 2 was the honesty job. Both had to ship on day one.
[engineering conversation · Q2 2024]

One panel. Three detection strategies.

Engineering surfaced the constraint that shaped the entire panel: Diagnostics can't work the same way for every content type.
strategy · play-based

Flows

A Flow is a sequence of steps. A broken step only reveals itself when the flow is triggered by an author or user action.

strategy · upfront

Smart Tips + Beacons + Launchers

Render on page load based on display rules. Detection can happen the moment the tab opens.

strategy · with care

Pop-ups

A Pop-up with occurrence: once won't re-fire on re-visit. Flagging that as a "failure" would misdiagnose correct behaviour.

strategy · upfront

Content on this URL

Whether content is scoped to the current URL is deterministic. Non-applicable content moves to Not on this page.

eng brought
The three-strategies constraint, named early. Without it we would have shipped a single "scan the page" button that misdiagnosed pop-ups and gave nothing on flows.
I brought
The UI reconciliation — one consistent tab per content type, with detection running the right way underneath. The complexity stays in the code, not on the surface.
trade-off named
Authors have to trigger flows to see step-level errors. That's exposed in the empty state for the Flows section: Play a flow to check its steps. Honest, not hidden.
[Figma + eng review · early Q3 2024]

Two ideas we removed.

Both looked right in the room. Both broke against the content-type constraint.
idea 1

A global list of all created content with issues attached.

Cut because content is contextual to a URL. An author needs to know where the issue lives, not just that it exists. A global list without URL context makes triage harder, not easier — it turns diagnosis into a scavenger hunt.

idea 2

A "Diagnose" button that scans the page and reports issues.

Cut because the button treats all content types the same. Scanning can't detect Flow step failures (they need play). Scanning Pop-ups with occurrence: once would produce false positives. One button couldn't respect three logics.

Cutting either was easier because engineering had named the constraints upfront. The cuts weren't taste; they were the honest engineering reality applied to the design. Both ideas would have shipped a tool that demoed well and misled authors at scale.

[Figma · design decisions · Q3 2024]

A tab inside Preview Mode.

Preview Mode was the surface. The next question was the container shape — modal, overlay, separate route, or tab. Each broke the loop differently.

Why a tab, not something else. A modal blocks the preview — the author can't check the content while diagnosing. An overlay obscures the page — the author can't see what they're diagnosing. A separate route breaks Studio's context — the author leaves the surface, which is the failure mode we'd just committed to avoiding. A tab sat alongside the preview, matched Studio's existing content-organization pattern, and let auto-navigation feel like part of the flow rather than an intrusion. Tab won on all four criteria: persistent, non-blocking, familiar, and native to Studio.

Five design decisions came out of that surface commitment.

  1. Diagnostics is a tab, not a separate surface. It lives inside Preview Mode. Enabling Preview Mode auto-navigates the author there — the assumption being: if you're previewing, you might be checking for issues.

  2. Categorized by content type, same as creation. Flows, Smart Tips, Beacons, Launchers, Pop-ups — the same taxonomy authors already knew from building. No new mental model to learn.

  3. Applicable content shows on the page; the rest goes to Not on this page. This makes the URL scoping explicit and answers Efraim's coverage question at the same time.

  4. Search by content type and issue type. A power-user path for authors managing many pieces at once.

  5. Notification banner for the minimized case. If Studio is minimized while previewing and a flow step fails, a banner surfaces at the top of the customer's browser. One click restores Studio to the Diagnostics tab, already opened to the failure.

[design walkthroughs · Q3 2024]

Two happy paths, one panel.

The Flow author reaches Diagnostics after a step fails during play. The Smart Tips author opens Diagnostics first — the issues are already visible.

Fixing a Flow step

strategy · play-based
  1. Create a flow.
  2. Enable Preview Mode.
  3. Play the flow.
  4. Step 3 fails.
  5. Open Diagnostics.
  6. Read the issue in creator language.
  7. Fix the step (if author-fixable).
  8. Save the flow.
  9. Re-preview to verify green.

Fixing a Smart Tip

strategy · upfront
  1. Create Smart Tips.
  2. Open Diagnostics.
  3. Open the Smart Tips accordion.
  4. See the failed ones surfaced upfront.
  5. Open one.
  6. Read the issue in creator language.
  7. Fix.
  8. Save.
  9. Re-preview to verify green.

Same panel. Two entry patterns, matching how each content type actually behaves. Same fix affordance underneath, so authors don't have to relearn the interaction when they move between content types.

[from deck · slide 48 · challenges named at project start]

What made this hard.

Three friction points visible from week one that shaped downstream decisions.

  1. Enterprise-customer recruiting was slow. 5–7 users per method was the ceiling, not the target. Most testing went to Muriel-side (L&D). Efraim's coverage view — a Phase 2 candidate — stayed thin on validation as a result.

  2. Engineering was split across two projects. Not all engineers attended every design review. The content-type strategy constraint surfaced from engineers who joined late; if it had surfaced in week two instead of week six, we would have saved a full design cycle.

  3. The design-system pod was mid-overhaul. They couldn't absorb a five-state status pill contribution on our timeline. Shipping the workaround was tactical; the DS pod shipped the proper primitive two months after launch.

The Support team's own read was clearer than any dashboard. Their per-ticket RCA time dropped — the diagnosis often arrived pre-attached to the escalation, so they were starting mid-investigation rather than at the top. The tickets that still came in had shifted toward Bucket 2, the technical issues authors couldn't fix themselves. Bucket 1 issues — display rules, selectors, stages, occurrence, page-scoping — mostly stopped arriving. Support went back to doing engineering work instead of translation work, which was the appreciation they voiced in the retro: the tickets we're getting are the ones we should have been getting all along.

Two quarters isn't enough to call the ticket drop durable. I'd want four before treating it as a baseline. The Support team outcome is the one I trust most — it's the read from the people who lived inside the old workflow every day.

[2026 reframing · what I'd unship, and what I'd build today]

What I'd unship, and what I'd design instead today.

Three things from the shipped product I'd change now:

  1. The auto-navigation to Diagnostics on Preview Mode enable — right assumption most of the time, wrong when the author is previewing routine changes and doesn't want a diagnostic surface. Should defer to a discoverable badge on first visit, auto-nav on second.

  2. The pill workaround — right tactical move, wrong long-term primitive. Co-design with DS earlier next time.

  3. The Pop-up occurrence-once handling — currently silent (we don't diagnose these). Better: surface an occurrence-satisfied state so authors understand why the pop-up isn't firing, without misdiagnosing it.

The larger honest note: the 2024 product is a tab inside Preview Mode that the author opens to inspect. In 2026, that shape is no longer the right one.

on-demand→ Continuous

Background watcher. The panel opens because the system invited it, not because the author suspected.

navigate to a tab→ Conversational

Why isn't my onboarding showing for new sales reps? The tab becomes one surface for the agent, not the product.

per-item→ Cross-content

Reason across the journey. This Flow fails because the Smart Tip before it stopped firing last week.

bucket 2 escalates→ The diagnosis is the ticket

Support reads the agent's analysis instead of running their own. Technical issues escalate with the RCA already done.

The 2024 product is a tab authors open. The 2026 product is an agent that opens the tab for them when it matters — and files the ticket itself when it doesn't.

Diagnostics taught me one thing that travelled to every project after: let the engineering constraint be the design brief. The three detection strategies weren't the enemy of the design — they were the shape of it. The panel that shipped is honest about that.
