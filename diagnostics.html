The second time we tried to build it.

Whatfix · 2024 · Senior PD · 0→1 (re-attempted) · Phase 1 shipped

tl;dr

Whatfix had failed at troubleshooting once before. Content Inspector — the year-old beta — had about ten weekly active users on a platform with thousands of seats. Diagnostics is the second attempt. I tested four concepts with customers before any code was written, validated the chosen direction at SUS 82, and shipped Phase 1 inside Preview Mode. In the first quarter post-launch, tier-1 tickets in the content-not-displaying category dropped about a third.

The failed first attempt.

By the time I started, Whatfix had already tried to solve this. Content Inspector — the beta meant to give creators self-service troubleshooting — had launched the year before. The Looker dashboard told me how it had landed.

In its place, a support model. A Whatfix engineer would dial into a customer's browser, run a debug command in the developer tools, and read the output aloud while the content creator watched.

Three other companies had built debuggers for the same job. None of them had agreed on the shape.

The brief I was handed in early 2024 — build an inspector panel in Studio — read like a respec of the failed beta. The first job was to understand why the last attempt had failed before deciding what to build next.

What discovery told us.

Eight weeks of customer interviews, ticket reads, and a second pass through two quarters of NPS verbatims. The Support team's own numbers were the first surprise — the cost of the problem was not on the customer's side of the table.

32 hrs
Average internal RCA time per ticket — Support analysing the issue and going back and forth with the customer.
182
Monthly tickets in the content-not-displaying category, across the customer base.
4
Parallel ways to test content existed inside Whatfix, none of them connected to each other.

Two primary users surfaced from the interviews. Same product, opposite vocabularies.

Muriel reasons in symptoms. Efraim reasons in coverage. The pain map separated what they shared from what diverged.

Two quarters of NPS verbatims, Q3 2023 and Q1 2024, returned the same complaint in different words. The Looker chart had counted the problem. The verbatims named it.

"There is no review mechanism built into Whatfix. When we proof each other's work as a final check before publishing, we screenshot and email feedback. A time-consuming process given the volume of content we create."

Customer admin · NPS verbatim, Q3 2023

How we picked a direction.

Three how-might-we framings on the wall: resolve faster, find the issue, notify and guide. Each one implied a different product, and only one was shippable in a quarter.

We picked find and resolve faster, in the moment of noticing. The verbatims said creators were already there when failures happened. They could not tell what had gone wrong.

Four concepts on the wall, before any code.

The previous attempt had shipped a single-concept MVP and refined against customer feedback after launch. Content Inspector was that approach. I wanted to test the model before we built it. Four concepts went up, each one a different bet about where the troubleshooting belonged.

Concept A

Diagnostics in Preview Mode

A passive inspector keyed to the current page. Indexes all five Whatfix content types. Creator opens preview, sees what's there and what isn't, edits without leaving the surface.

upside — covers every content type, in-context editing, low cross-surface switching.
downside — technical failures still escape to Support; the right rail is space-constrained.

Concept B

Step-level diagnostics during play

A creator runs a flow; the panel marks each step pass or fail with the failure reason inline. No cross-platform switching; edits land in real time.

upside — sharpest signal for the Flow content type; clear next-step guidance.
downside — only works for Flows. The other four content types are configured, not played.

Concept C

In-Studio ticketing with screen recording

Instead of self-resolution, faster escalation. A Report Issue flow inside Studio that bundles a screen recording and console logs for Support.

upside — slashes Support's 32-hour RCA time; preserves the existing mental model.
downside — privacy concerns with screen capture; does not move creators toward self-service.

Concept D

Diagnostics in Dashboard

A global failure list inside the existing dashboard. Admins triage at scale, drill into any failed asset, mark fixed.

upside — coverage view for Efraim's persona; clear resolution path; aligned with audit mental models.
downside — abstract from the moment of failure; large lists become their own burden.

The first concept won the test, but the conviction came from how the four were ranked together. C covered Support's pain. D covered Efraim. B was the sharpest experience for one content type. A was the only one that put the answer where the creator was already looking. We took A into Phase 1 with the strongest C and D capabilities queued behind it.

Testing the concepts before we built one.

The four concepts went to customers as clickable prototypes. This was the first project at Whatfix where I tested concepts before committing to development — the prior pattern was ship an MVP and learn from production. The case for testing first was a fast one to make: Content Inspector's chart was on the wall.

Sample
5–7 customers per concept, enterprise segment, all with one-plus month of DAP experience, English speakers.
Methods
Moderated walkthroughs and post-task surveys, over two weeks.
What we tested
Could they reach the right concept for their problem? Did the reasoning we surfaced match what they expected to read?
Outcome
Concept A advanced. Concept C's ticketing was retained as a fast-follow. B and D shaped the Phase 2 backlog.

Four themes came out of the testing that shaped the final design.

  1. Creators loved the in-context reasoning. When the panel said this Pop-up does not appear because its segment does not match the current URL, they read it like a sentence and reached for the edit affordance. The phrasing was the affordance.

  2. The ticketing flow worked, with a caveat. Creators understood the screen-recording capture immediately. They also flagged sensitive data on the recorded page — which moved the ticketing flow behind a redaction step in Phase 2.

  3. Technical-sounding reasons triggered immediate escalation. A reason that read as code or system state — parent_segment_mismatch — was a signal to creators that they should not try and should raise a ticket. This validated the question-framed translation layer for the panel.

  4. Self-resolution was the dominant want. Creators reported they would try once on their own before reaching for Support — and that the panel's reasoning was clear enough to do so.

The surface decision.

The first flow diagram had three entry points — Studio, Preview Mode, Dashboard — feeding into a single Debugging panel. It was approved in twenty minutes. Two weeks later, I asked to take it down.

In Studio, a creator is building. They want to know whether the element they are about to attach to is stable. In Preview, they are checking their work. In Dashboard, they are triaging at scale. Each one is a different question and a different product. The replacement was a single-surface proposal: Preview Mode for Phase 1, Studio and Dashboard deferred.

The call
Ship Preview Mode only in Phase 1. Studio and Dashboard deferred to Phase 2 and 3.
Pushback · PM
Wanted all three for the Sales kickoff slide. The planned narrative was diagnostics everywhere.
Pushback · Eng
Believed Studio was the natural home — closer to the existing content editor, lower integration risk.
How I worked it
Wrote a three-page memo. Made the case that shipping one well beats shipping three thinly. Cited Content Inspector's adoption chart as the cost of the thinly-shipped version.
Outcome
PM signed off in round three. Engineering signed off after we agreed Phase 2 would start the following quarter.

The model pivot.

Three sprints in, the interaction model was play-driven. A creator would run a flow, and the inspector would show step-by-step results. It worked. For one of the five Whatfix content types.

→

Smart Tips, Beacons, Launchers, and Pop-ups are configured, not played. The play model never reached them. The rewrite was a passive inspector keyed to the current page: open the panel and see what is there, and what should be there but isn't.

The call
Switch from a play-driven sequence to a passive inspector keyed to the current page.
Trigger
Realised the play model only saw Flows. The other four content types were configured, not played.
Pushback · Eng
The v1 UI was three sprints along. Engineering proposed shipping v1 with v2 as fast-follow.
Why I held
v1 would have shipped a tool that worked for 20% of the content types it was named for. Same failure mode as Content Inspector.
Cost
Three sprints rewritten. Six weeks of timeline absorbed.

One more test before launch.

Two weeks before code-freeze, the passive inspector went through a moderated usability test. The goal was narrow: confirm creators could complete the four critical tasks unsupervised and that the panel did not introduce confusion that the concept test had not surfaced.

Sample
5–7 enterprise customers, one-plus month of DAP experience, English speakers — same recruitment criteria as concept testing, fresh participants.
Methods
Task-based usability test with think-aloud, over two-to-three weeks.
SUS score
82. Above the 80 threshold for an A grade. Participants completed all four tasks without facilitator intervention.
Headline finding
"Inactive" as a status label was read as an action verb, not a state. We renamed it to not displaying with the failure reason exposed on the same line.

The test was scoped against the constraint, not the optimism. We did not ask whether the panel was delightful. We asked whether a creator who had never seen it could finish the four tasks the product was named for.

What shipped.

Phase 1 shipped in late 2024. Diagnostics lives in Preview Mode as a right-side drawer. It indexes the five Whatfix content types on the current page — Flows, Smart Tips, Beacons, Launchers, Pop-ups — and surfaces each with a status. Two tabs: on this page and not on this page. The second is the sleeper feature. Most diagnostic tools only show problems with what is there. This one also shows what should be there and isn't.

The default tab is on this page. The bet was that creators check their own work more often than they search for what's missing. A creator who scans down the list and sees green is done. The not on this page tab exists for the harder case, where a creator is looking for content they expected to find and can't. The tab that opens first is the design.

The detail panel uses question framing instead of attribute framing. Where engineering wanted a flat attribute table — display_order: 1 — the panel asks When does this appear? and answers in a sentence. The raw state is one click into the accordion.

The call
Question-framed answers in the default view. Raw state in an accordion, one click deeper.
Pushback · Eng
Wanted a flat attribute table — faithful read of the underlying state. Would also have served internal Support without a second UI.
How I worked it
Mapped a sample of Support tickets to find the question creators were actually asking. When does this appear? won. display_order: 1 was the wrong unit.
Outcome
Got both. Questions are the default; raw state is one click. The translation layer is ongoing maintenance.

The panel composes from existing patterns. The right-side drawer is Studio's content editor. The status pills are the Analytics dashboard's. The accordion is the same primitive that Workflows uses. No new visual language was introduced and no new documentation page was needed on the customer side.

The first-run tooltip is one anchored hint, dismissed on first click. We did not build a tour. The bet was that the panel would earn its place on the second session, not the first — and a tour would have slowed the second session more than it would have helped the first.

Phase 1, and what's queued behind it.

The roadmap was the negotiation. The Sales kickoff narrative wanted everything. The team needed to ship one good surface. The phasing made what got cut visible to everyone who signed off.

  • Step-level diagnostics for Flows

The Phase 1 trade-off that hurt most internally: Report Issue was held back. Concept testing had validated it; the engineering work to bundle a redacted recording with console logs needed its own quarter. Customer Success carried that gap for two sprints after launch.

What the first two quarters said.

−33%
Tier-1 tickets in the content-not-displaying category, Q1 post-launch, baselined against the prior six months. Held in Q2.
−20%
Screen-share requests in the same category. The smaller drop suggests the calls were never only about diagnosis.
82
SUS score from pre-launch usability testing — above the A-grade threshold of 80.
0
Net-new UI patterns. The panel reuses Whatfix's existing drawer, pill, and accordion components.

Two quarters of data is not enough to call the ticket drop durable. I would want four before treating it as a baseline.

What I'd unship.

  1. The Diagnose CTA. It opens a panel that answers why, well — and doesn't help a creator fix. I would rename it Inspect, or add the missing affordance behind it.

  2. The phasing without a date. Studio and Dashboard were deferred without a public commitment. Eighteen months in, the answer to when is still yes, coming.

  3. The naming concession. Sales had used diagnostics in pitch decks before launch. I should have asked them to hold the name until the product shipped.

Where the process strained.

The shipped product is what it is. The process underneath had three friction points worth naming — not because they broke the project, but because they would have produced a better one if I'd resolved them sooner.

  1. Recruiting across personas was the timeline bottleneck. Concept testing and usability testing each needed enterprise customers with one-plus month of DAP experience. The recruitment pipeline could not produce both Muriel-style and Efraim-style participants on schedule. We ran most testing against the Muriel side. Efraim's coverage view in Phase 2 will need its own usability validation before commit.

  2. Fragmented engineering attendance produced late opinions. Engineers were split across two concurrent projects. Critical design reviews happened with partial engineering present, which meant the model pivot in week eight surfaced after engineers who had not been in earlier reviews caught up. A standing eng-design weekly with the same three names would have caught it in week three.

  3. Design-system adherence cost us two better treatments. The status pill component supported three states; Diagnostics needed five. We extended it sideways with copy and shape variation instead of designing the proper component, because the design-system pod was mid-overhaul and could not absorb a contribution. Two months after launch, the design-system team flagged the same gap from their side. The right move is co-design earlier in the cycle.

What I'd close before Phase 2.

Three places the process did not engage. They are not faults of the shipped product. They are work that didn't get done — and that I would close before any Phase 2 commit.

  1. The AI translation layer. The question-framed answers in the detail panel are hand-authored — one engineer wrote each translation. In 2024, an LLM could have generated those from raw state at lower maintenance cost. We did not build it that way because deterministic translations gave us a quality floor the team could verify. The right call for Phase 1. The wrong default for Phase 2 if the maintenance cost keeps growing.

  2. Performance against real customer pages. Preview Mode runs as a browser extension. The panel inspects the page DOM on open. We set a 200ms budget to first paint and met it on internal test fixtures. We did not measure against real customer pages with thousands of DOM nodes, or against pages with heavy iframe content. That work is outstanding.

  3. Accessibility verification. The panel has keyboard navigation and focus management. Status uses both color and shape, not color alone. We did not run a formal WCAG 2.2 audit before launch — compliance is asserted by the team, not third-party verified. A pre-Phase-2 audit is the cheapest reduction in risk we can buy.

What I took from it.

This was the first project at Whatfix where I tested concepts before development instead of after, and the first where I tied design-driven work to a roadmap through measurable Key Results. The pattern travelled. When I began leading a platform team after this, KR-first roadmap planning was the default. The case study I wrote about Diagnostics was the artefact that taught me to write one.
