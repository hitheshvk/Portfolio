Diagnostics.

Whatfix had tried to build this once. Ten people used it. Here is what the second attempt looked like, what it could not yet prove, and what I would build instead today.

Whatfix · 2024, written in 2026 · Senior PD · 0→1, second attempt · Phase 1 shipped

Diagnostics is a passive troubleshooting tool inside Whatfix Preview Mode. I tested four concepts with customers before any code was written, killed three sprints of work in week eight, and shipped Phase 1 with the panel sized to one surface instead of three. In the first quarter post-launch, tier-1 tickets in the content-not-displaying category dropped by about a third. Eighteen months later, the product is correct for 2024 and insufficient for 2026 — and that gap is what I want to write about most.

Ten weekly active users.

10 on a platform with thousands of seats. The chart was on page two of the planning doc, where bad news goes when leadership has chosen to keep funding the problem anyway.

That was the active-user count on the year-old Content Inspector beta when I picked up the brief in early 2024. The brief read like a respec of the same beta. The first job was to understand why the last attempt had failed before deciding what to build next.

In its place, a support model. A Whatfix engineer would dial into a customer's browser, run a debug command in the developer tools, and read the output aloud while the content creator watched.

Three other companies had built debuggers for the same job. None of them had agreed on the shape — which told me the right answer was not yet a commodity, and that the brief I was holding was the most expensive part of the project to leave unexamined.

The cost was on the inside.

Eight weeks of customer interviews and ticket reads turned up a number that changed who the product was for.
32 hrs
Average internal RCA time per ticket — Support analysing the issue and going back and forth with the customer.
182
Monthly tickets in the content-not-displaying category, across the customer base.
4
Parallel ways to test content existed inside Whatfix, none of them connected to each other.

The 32-hour number was the unlock. The problem wasn't only that customers were frustrated. It was that every customer ticket became a Support engineer's day. At 182 monthly tickets averaging 32 hours each, the team was burning roughly 30 person-weeks per quarter on RCA work that should have been the product's job.

The original brief — build a self-service inspector for content creators — was correct, but it had been pitched as a customer-experience improvement. The faster, harder pitch was an internal-cost reduction with a customer-experience improvement attached.

Same product, opposite vocabularies. Muriel asks why is my work broken. Efraim asks where in my library is something quietly breaking. Both questions needed an answer. Neither persona was getting one.

"There is no review mechanism built into Whatfix. When we proof each other's work as a final check before publishing, we screenshot and email feedback. A time-consuming process given the volume of content we create."

Customer admin · NPS verbatim, Q3 2023

Four concepts on the wall, before any code.

The previous attempt had assumed Studio was the right surface, built it, and learned in production that it wasn't. The surface decision became the test.
Concept A

Diagnostics in Preview Mode

A passive inspector keyed to the current page. Indexes all five Whatfix content types. Creator opens preview, sees what's there and what isn't, edits without leaving the surface.

upside — covers every content type, in-context editing, low cross-surface switching.
downside — technical failures still escape to Support; the right rail is space-constrained.

Concept B

Step-level diagnostics during play

A creator runs a flow; the panel marks each step pass or fail with the failure reason inline. No cross-platform switching; edits land in real time.

upside — sharpest signal for the Flow content type; clear next-step guidance.
downside — only works for Flows. The other four content types are configured, not played.

Concept C

In-Studio ticketing with screen capture

Instead of self-resolution, faster escalation. A Report Issue flow inside Studio that bundles a screen recording and console logs for Support.

upside — slashes Support's 32-hour RCA time; preserves the existing mental model.
downside — privacy concerns with screen capture; does not move creators toward self-service.

Concept D

Diagnostics in Dashboard

A global failure list inside the existing dashboard. Admins triage at scale, drill into any failed asset, mark fixed.

upside — coverage view for Efraim's persona; clear resolution path; aligned with audit mental models.
downside — abstract from the moment of failure; large lists become their own burden.

This was the first project at Whatfix where I tested concepts before development. The prior pattern was ship-an-MVP-and-learn-from-production. The cost of that pattern was the ten-active-user chart.

Sample
5–7 enterprise customers per concept, one-plus month of DAP experience, English speakers.
Methods
Moderated walkthroughs with clickable prototypes plus post-task surveys, over two weeks.
What we tested
Could creators reach the right concept for their problem? Did the reasoning we surfaced match what they expected to read?
Outcome
Concept A advanced. Concept C's ticketing was retained as a fast-follow. B and D shaped the Phase 2 backlog.

Four themes came out of the testing that shaped the final design.

  1. Creators read the in-context reasoning as a sentence and reached for the edit affordance. The phrasing was the affordance. Status pills alone were not enough; status pills plus a sentence were.

  2. Self-resolution was the dominant want. Creators reported they would try once on their own before reaching for Support — provided the reasoning was clear enough to do so.

  3. Technical-sounding reasons triggered immediate escalation. A reason that read as code or system state — parent_segment_mismatch — was a signal to creators that they should not try. This validated the question-framed translation layer.

  4. The ticketing flow worked, with a caveat. Creators flagged sensitive data on the recorded page, which moved Report Issue behind a redaction step in Phase 2.

Two hard calls.

If either had gone the other way, we would have shipped a worse product. Both took weeks to land.

The surface decision. The first flow diagram had three entry points — Studio, Preview Mode, Dashboard — feeding into a single Debugging panel. It was approved in twenty minutes. Two weeks later, I asked to take it down.

In Studio, a creator is building. They want to know whether the element they are about to attach to is stable. In Preview, they are checking their work. In Dashboard, they are triaging at scale. Each one is a different question and a different product.

The call
Ship Preview Mode only in Phase 1. Studio and Dashboard deferred to Phase 2 and 3.
Pushback · PM
Wanted all three for the Sales kickoff slide. The planned narrative was diagnostics everywhere.
Pushback · Eng
Believed Studio was the natural home — closer to the existing content editor, lower integration risk.
How I worked it
Wrote a three-page memo. Cited Content Inspector's adoption chart as the cost of the thinly-shipped version. PM signed off in round three; Eng signed off when we committed Phase 2 to the next quarter.

The model pivot. Three sprints in, the interaction model was play-driven. A creator would run a flow; the inspector would show step-by-step results. It worked for one of the five Whatfix content types. Smart Tips, Beacons, Launchers, and Pop-ups are configured, not played. The play model never reached them.

The call
Switch from a play-driven sequence to a passive inspector keyed to the current page.
Pushback · Eng
v1 UI was three sprints along. Engineering proposed shipping v1 with v2 as fast-follow.
Why I held
v1 would have shipped a tool that worked for 20% of the content types it was named for. Same failure mode as Content Inspector.
Cost
Three sprints rewritten. Six weeks of timeline absorbed.

What shipped.

Phase 1 shipped in late 2024. Diagnostics lives in Preview Mode as a right-side drawer. It indexes the five Whatfix content types on the current page — Flows, Smart Tips, Beacons, Launchers, Pop-ups — and surfaces each with a status. Two tabs: on this page and not on this page. The second is the sleeper feature. Most diagnostic tools only show problems with what is there. This one also shows what should be there and isn't.

The default tab is on this page. The bet was that creators check their own work more often than they search for what's missing. The tab that opens first is the design.

The detail panel uses question framing instead of attribute framing. Where engineering wanted a flat attribute table — display_order: 1 — the panel asks When does this appear? and answers in a sentence. The raw state is one click into the accordion.

The panel composes from existing patterns. The right-side drawer is Studio's content editor. The status pills are the Analytics dashboard's. The accordion is the same primitive that Workflows uses. No new visual language was introduced and no new documentation page was needed on the customer side.

The Phase 1 trade-off that hurt most internally: Report Issue was held back. Concept testing had validated it; the engineering work to bundle a redacted recording with console logs needed its own quarter. Customer Success carried that gap for two sprints after launch.

What the first two quarters said.

−33%
Tier-1 tickets in the content-not-displaying category, Q1 post-launch, baselined against the prior six months. Held in Q2.
−20%
Screen-share requests in the same category. The smaller drop suggests the calls were never only about diagnosis.
82

0
Net-new UI patterns. The panel reuses Whatfix's existing drawer, pill, and accordion components.

Two quarters of data is not enough to call the ticket drop durable. I would want four before treating it as a baseline.

What I'd unship.

  1. The Diagnose CTA. It opens a panel that answers why, well — and doesn't help a creator fix. I would rename it Inspect, or add the missing affordance behind it.

  2. The phasing without a date. Studio and Dashboard were deferred without a public commitment. Eighteen months in, the answer to when is still yes, coming.

  3. The naming concession. Sales had used diagnostics in pitch decks before launch. I should have asked them to hold the name until the product shipped.

What this case study can't yet prove.

A senior reader should hold the receipts and the gaps in equal weight. Here is the evidence I don't have.

  1. Business-level outcome. Tickets dropped a third. I don't have revenue impact, retention impact, or expansion impact for accounts that adopted the panel. The Support-cost reduction is real; the upstream value is uncounted.

  2. Multi-quarter durability. The −33% held in Q2. I have not re-run the query for Q3 or Q4. The drop may have decayed as the long tail of harder issues filled the space the easy ones vacated.

  3. Competitive lift. Whatfix sells against Pendo, Appcues, and WalkMe. Diagnostics is the only one of the four built into Preview Mode by default. I do not have data on whether sales cycles have shifted because of it.

  4. Cross-persona evidence. Most concept and usability testing went to the Muriel side. Efraim's coverage view is queued for Phase 2 and has not been validated with the depth Phase 1 was.

  5. Customer voice post-launch. I have NPS verbatims from before. I do not have a clustered read of what customers are saying about the panel eighteen months in.

Each of these is a question I would ask if I were the hiring manager. I am naming them because they are real.

the 2026 lens

What I'd build instead today.

The model I shipped is a panel a creator opens when they suspect a problem. In 2026, that is no longer the right shape.

The constraints that justified the 2024 design have softened. LLM reliability for customer-facing reasoning crossed the threshold where deterministic verification can be the audit trail rather than the front line. The browser-extension infrastructure to monitor content health continuously is now standard. Design teams operating at this altitude in 2026 do not ship a tool — they ship a model of the customer's content that the tool happens to surface.

2024 — on-demand → Continuous

Watch content health in the background. Surface issues to the creator before the creator notices them. The panel opens because the system invited it, not because the creator suspected something.

2024 — navigational → Conversational

Why isn't my onboarding flow showing for new sales reps? A creator asks the question; the system reasons across content, segments, and rules to answer. The panel becomes one surface for the agent, not the product.

2024 — per-item → Cross-content

Each piece of content is diagnosed in isolation today. The better model reasons across the journey — this Flow fails because the Smart Tip that preceded it stopped firing last week.

2024 — reactive → Predictive

Tell creators what is about to break, based on patterns from their own content history and from the cohort. Drift detection on the things creators do not check often enough to catch.

2024 — open a ticket → The diagnosis is the ticket

When Support is needed, the system has already done the RCA. Support reads the agent's analysis instead of running their own. The 32-hour number stops being a Support problem.

The 2024 product was a panel. The 2026 product is an agent that uses a panel when it needs one.

The honest version: the AI translation layer I deferred to Phase 2 is the first lever. If I were starting today, that layer would be the foundation, not the optional enhancement — and everything above sits on top of it.

Where this goes next.

A three-year arc for the product, written backwards from what I want a creator to never have to do.

Year 1

now → end of 2026

Phase 2 ships. Report Issue with redacted screen capture. Failed-in-production tab. The AI translation layer replaces hand-authored reasoning. Foundations for everything below.

Year 2

2027

Continuous monitoring. The agent watches content health in the background. Proactive notifications for at-risk content. Cross-content reasoning. The panel becomes a surface, not the product.

Year 3

2028

Predictive layer. Cross-tenant pattern recognition. Diagnostics becomes the foundation of Whatfix's always-on content layer. A creator never opens the panel because they never need to.

The endpoint is a creator who never opens the panel because the system told them what to fix, when, and why, in a sentence. Phase 1 made it cheap to know what was broken. Phase 2 makes it cheap to act. Years 2 and 3 make the asking unnecessary.

Where the process strained.

Three friction points worth naming — not because they broke the project, but because they would have produced a better one.

  1. Recruiting across personas was the timeline bottleneck. Concept testing and usability testing each needed enterprise customers with one-plus month of DAP experience. The pipeline could not produce both Muriel-style and Efraim-style participants on schedule. We ran most testing against the Muriel side. Efraim's coverage view in Phase 2 will need its own validation before commit.

  2. Fragmented engineering attendance produced late opinions. Engineers were split across two concurrent projects. Critical design reviews happened with partial engineering present. The model pivot in week eight surfaced after engineers who had not been in earlier reviews caught up. A standing eng-design weekly with the same three names would have caught it in week three.

  3. Design-system adherence cost us two better treatments. The status pill component supported three states; Diagnostics needed five. We extended sideways with copy and shape variation instead of designing the proper component, because the design-system pod was mid-overhaul and could not absorb a contribution. Two months after launch, the design-system team flagged the same gap from their side. Co-design earlier in the cycle is the fix.

What I'd close before Phase 2.

Three places the process did not engage. Not faults of the shipped product. Work that didn't get done.

  1. The AI translation layer. Question-framed answers are hand-authored — one engineer wrote each translation. In 2024 deterministic translations gave a quality floor the team could verify. In 2026 the floor is higher and the maintenance is no longer optional. This is Phase 2's first job, not its last.

  2. Performance against real customer pages. We set a 200ms budget to first paint and met it on internal fixtures. We did not measure against real customer pages with thousands of DOM nodes or heavy iframe content. Outstanding.

  3. Accessibility verification. The panel has keyboard navigation and focus management. Status uses both color and shape. We did not run a formal WCAG 2.2 audit before launch — compliance is asserted by the team, not third-party verified. The cheapest risk reduction available.

What I took from it.

This was the first project at Whatfix where I tested concepts before development instead of after, and the first where I tied design-driven work to a roadmap through measurable KRs. The pattern travelled. When I began leading a platform team after this, KR-first roadmap planning was the default.

The harder takeaway is the one I am still writing. The product I shipped is correct for 2024 and insufficient for 2026. That gap — looking at what shipped through the lens of what is now possible, and asking whether the answer that was right two years ago is still the right answer now — is the design work that should never stop.

Diagnostics is the artefact that taught me to write a case study. The 2026 lens is the one I am still writing.
