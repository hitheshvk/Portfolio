One editor, five content types, and personas who'd never touched a CMS

Whatfix · 2025 · Senior PD · 0→1 · Shipped

TL;DR

Whatfix's content-creation surface had grown one content type at a time: Flows, Pop-ups, Tooltips, Beacons and Launchers each had their own entry point, their own mental model, and their own onboarding. Non-technical authors bounced between them without a shared vocabulary for "what am I building and why." Studio consolidates all five into one guided editor (Guide, Show, Ask, Analyze) with a single scenario-or-type starting point. Post-launch: 100% self-servicability in testing and an 84 SUS.

The problem

Five content types, five different front doors.

Flows, Pop-ups, Tooltips, Beacons and Launchers had shipped at different times, by different sub-teams, each with its own creation flow. They did related jobs: surfacing guidance or information inside a customer's product, but nothing about the surfaces suggested that relationship. An author who'd mastered Tooltips started from zero on Pop-ups.

Support saw the cost first: onboarding questions weren't about any single content type, they were about the seams between them. "Which one do I use for X" was the most common ticket category we couldn't route to a single owner, because the honest answer depended on the author's intent, not the content type.

Concept

Start from intent, not from content type.

The reframe was to stop asking authors to pick a content type first. Studio opens on a single choice: Scenario or Type, so an author who knows what they're trying to accomplish ("guide a new user through setup") can get there without first learning that the answer is "a Flow." An author who already knows the primitive they want can still jump straight to Type.

Above that choice sits a four-tab structure (Guide, Show, Ask, Analyze) that reframes the whole surface around what an author is doing with content, not which primitive they're editing: building it, previewing it, asking questions about it, or measuring it.

What shipped

Readymade content as the default door.

The highest-friction part of the old flow was the blank canvas: an author staring at an empty Flow editor with no sense of what a good one looked like. Studio's default entry is Readymade content: a curated list an author can start from and edit, rather than build from nothing. Authors who wanted to build from scratch still could, one click deeper.

Show, Ask and Analyze became the other three legs of the same surface: preview content in context, ask questions about existing content instead of hunting through settings, and see performance without leaving the editor. None of the three were new capabilities. They existed elsewhere in the product. Studio's job was consolidation, not invention.

Outcomes & reflection

100% self-servicability, and a question I'd ask earlier next time.

Every author in the post-launch usability cohort completed their task without escalating: 100% self-servicability against a baseline where the old flow regularly required a support touchpoint for first-time authors. The redesigned flow scored 84 on SUS, and the qualitative read from customers matched the number: authors described the surface as one thing to learn, not five.

What I'd revisit: I validated the Scenario/Type split with authors who already knew Whatfix. I'd want a cold-start cohort: people who'd never opened the old creation flow, to stress-test whether "Scenario" reads as clearly to someone with zero prior mental model as it did to someone unlearning one.
