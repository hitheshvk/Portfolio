Diagnostics.

Whatfix's first troubleshooting tool earned 10 weekly users. This is the design that shipped the second one — and what would make it right today.

Whatfix · 2024, written in 2026 · Senior PD · 0→1 (second attempt) · Phase 1 shipped

Studio's Preview Mode was where creators already went to check their work — research picked the surface before design did. Diagnostics is the panel I put there. Five months of design, three arguments that could have gone the other way, and one measurable win: tickets down about a third, holding.

The team: a designer (me), a PM, an EM, and a UX researcher on-call. Roughly ten cross-pod partners across Support, Sales, and the Design System pod. This case study credits each of them where their contribution shaped the shipped work.

[from PRD · Content Inspector adoption chart]

Ten weekly active users.

10 on a platform with thousands of seats. The chart was on page two of the planning doc, where bad news goes when leadership has chosen to keep funding the problem anyway.

Content Inspector — Whatfix's first troubleshooting tool — had launched the year before. The brief I picked up in early 2024 read like a respec of it. The first design job was to throw out the brief.

Support's workaround was manual: an engineer dialled into the customer's browser, ran a debug command in the developer tools, and read the output aloud while the creator watched. Fastest tool Whatfix had. Unusable by anyone who hadn't been trained on it.

[from deck · discovery research + PRD baselines]

The brief was off by an audience.

Eight weeks of interviews and ticket reads turned up the numbers that reframed who the product was actually for.
32 hrs
Internal RCA time per ticket.
182
Monthly tickets in the content-not-displaying category.
4
Ways to preview content — only one used at scale.

The brief was pitched as customer experience. Support's math flipped it: 182 monthly tickets × 32 hours each ≈ 30 person-weeks per quarter of RCA the product should have been doing.

The research also answered a question the brief hadn't asked. Of the four ways to preview content in Whatfix — Preview Mode, staging URLs, the dev-console command, publish-and-check — only Preview Mode was used at scale. That finding picked the surface before design did.

[from deck · slide 24]

Three how-might-we's on the wall.

Each implied a different product. Only one was shippable in a quarter.

  1. HMW help a creator resolve any technical issue faster — a broader troubleshooting suite than the quarter would fit.

  2. HMW help a creator find the issue and resolve it in the moment of noticing — the shape of a Phase-1-shaped product.

  3. HMW notify a creator about failures and guide them to resolve — a monitoring layer we didn't yet have.

We picked HMW 2. NPS verbatims said creators were already there when failures happened; they just couldn't tell what had gone wrong.

[2026 reframing · flows redrawn from interviews and shipped product]

32 hours today. Minutes tomorrow.

Current-state and future-state flows, drawn to make the design bet visible. Collapsing the middle from days to minutes.

Current state

2023 · from creation to fix ~32 hrs
  1. Creator creates content.
  2. Notices content isn't displaying as expected.
  3. Tries to reproduce; can't isolate the cause.
  4. Files a Support ticket.
  5. Waits for Support to schedule a screen-share.
  6. Support runs _wfx_debug_panel(); reads the output aloud.
  7. Creator edits offline based on what they heard.
  8. Publishes to verify; catches surprises in production.

Path we designed for

2024 · from creation to fix, inside Preview ~minutes
  1. Creator creates content.
  2. Enables Preview Mode to preview the created content.
  3. Notices content isn't displaying as expected.
  4. Panel indexes the page; surfaces failed items.
  5. Creator scans statuses; opens the failed one.
  6. Reads the answer in creator language.
  7. Edits inline, still inside Preview.
  8. Verifies in Preview; publishes when green.

The important shift isn't just fewer steps — it's where the creator is when they notice the problem. In the current state, the failure surfaces after publish (or during a broken test); the creator has already left the making-mode. In the future state, previewing is a routine step of creation itself, and the panel is already indexing when the eye catches the missing pop-up. Everything downstream — the diagnosis, the edit, the re-verify — happens without leaving the surface.

[from deck · slides 29–32]

Four concepts, tested before code.

Preview Mode was the surface. Concepts A and B tested how diagnostics lived inside it; C and D tested alternatives elsewhere.
A

Preview Mode · passive

Passive panel inside Studio's Preview Mode. Indexes all five content types on the page.

B

Preview Mode · play-driven

Step-level diagnostics as a flow runs. Sharpest for Flows; blind to the other four types.

C

Studio editor · ticketing

In-editor Report Issue with bundled recording. Speeds Support, not creators.

D

Dashboard · coverage

Global failure list for admins. Coverage view for Efraim; abstract from the moment of failure.

Five to seven enterprise customers tested each concept as clickable prototypes. Concept A advanced. C was queued for Phase 2. B and D shaped the backlog. The strongest concept-test finding: creators read in-context reasoning as a sentence and reached for the edit affordance — the phrasing was the affordance.

[from deck · slide 48 · named at project start, not after]

What made this hard.

Three constraints that were visible from week one. Each shaped a design decision downstream.

  1. Enterprise-customer recruiting was slow. 5–7 users per concept-test method was the ceiling, not the target. Most testing went to Muriel-side (L&D). Efraim (Info Developer) coverage stayed thin — which is why Phase 2's Dashboard view is still unvalidated.

  2. Engineering was split across two projects. Not all engineers attended every design review. The model pivot in week eight surfaced from engineers who'd joined late and hadn't been part of earlier concept discussions.

  3. The design-system pod was mid-overhaul. They couldn't absorb a five-state status pill contribution on our timeline. Shipping the workaround was tactical; the system-level fix came from them two months after launch.

[from Figma · component iterations · shipped Q4 2024]

Three components, three arguments.

The design system had three-state pills, four-tab conventions, and a preference for attribute tables. Diagnostics needed five states, two tabs, and question-framed answers. Each was a negotiation.

Tabs — from four to two.

Draft, Ready, Published, All — the workflow-stage tabs matched Whatfix conventions and answered the wrong question. Diagnostics asks is what's here working? or what should be here but isn't? Workflow stage is orthogonal to either.

→
researcher noticed
In concept-test session three, a creator asked, unprompted, about a Smart Tip she expected to see and couldn't. That question was the seed for Not on this page.
PM pushed for
Keeping workflow-stage tabs — Sales wanted to demo them for the "matches Studio conventions" pitch. Countered by moving stages to a chip filter, which preserved the affordance without owning tab real estate.
shipped
Two question tabs, one chip filter for stages. Both audiences got what they needed; the panel got the axis right.

Status pill — three states became five.

The existing Whatfix pill supported Active / Inactive / Draft. Diagnostics needed displaying, not-displaying-rule-mismatch, not-displaying-element-missing, error, loading. Colour alone would have failed WCAG for colour-vision differences.

DS pod raised
Their pillar refactor was mid-cycle. They could not merge a five-state contribution on our timeline — the honest constraint, named early.
I proposed
Ship a tactical extension (shape variation on the existing pill) for Phase 1. File the gap as a formal contribution ask.
DS shipped later
Two months after launch, the DS pod merged a proper five-state primitive that retroactively covered Diagnostics. Right escalation, right sequence.

Detail panel — question, not table.

Engineering's first request was a flat attribute table — display_order: 1, segment_match: true. Faithful to system state; would also serve internal Support. Creators could not read it.

eng brought
A valid audit-trail requirement — internal Support needed raw attribute state to debug complex tickets. Losing it would have created a second UI to build.
I brought
A sample of Support tickets showing what creators actually asked. Most started with why, when, or where — questions an attribute table can't answer in the reader's language.
we landed on
Question-framed answer as the default. Raw state in an accordion, auto-opened for internal Support roles. Same data, two readers, one component.
[shipped · Q4 2024]

What shipped.

Phase 1 lives inside Studio's Preview Mode as a right-side drawer. Two tabs, five content types, creator-language status, question-framed detail. The panel opens with Preview Mode — the same gesture creators already used to check their work, now with an answer key attached. Zero net-new UI patterns for customers to learn: the drawer is Studio's, the pills are Analytics', the accordion is Workflows'.

[2026 reframing · what I'd unship, and what I'd design instead today]

What I'd unship, and what I'd build instead today.

Three from the shipped product I'd change now:

  1. The Diagnose CTA — it opens a panel that answers why, well, but doesn't help a creator fix. Rename to Inspect, or add the missing affordance.

  2. The pill workaround — right tactical move, wrong long-term primitive. Co-design with DS earlier next time.

  3. The first-run tooltip on every account — fires for power users who don't need it. Should suppress on second-session Preview Mode usage.

The larger honest note: the 2024 product is a panel a creator opens when they suspect a problem. In 2026, that shape is no longer the right one.

on-demand→ Continuous

Background watcher. The panel opens because the system invited it, not because the creator suspected.

point and click→ Conversational

Why isn't my onboarding showing for new sales reps? The panel becomes one surface for the agent, not the product.

per-item→ Cross-content

Reason across the journey. This Flow fails because the Smart Tip before it stopped firing last week.

open a ticket→ The diagnosis is the ticket

Support reads the agent's analysis instead of running their own. The 32-hour number stops being a Support problem.

The 2024 product was a panel. The 2026 product is an agent that uses a panel when it needs one. The LLM reasoning layer I deferred to Phase 2 is the first lever — foundation, not enhancement.

Diagnostics taught me to test the concept before the MVP, defend the design with the user's own words, and bring the design system in before the workaround. The 2026 lens is the one I'm still writing.
